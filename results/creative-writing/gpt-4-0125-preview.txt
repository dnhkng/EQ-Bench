Fairy Tale Retelling: Rewrite the story of Hansel and Gretel from the perspective of the witch, in the format of raw, terse stream-of-consciousness diary entries written in her style & voice. Include snippets of dialogue between the witch and the children in a way that feels natural for a diary entry. You may take liberties with the original story. The witch will not die in this version; she needs to be able to write her final entry. It will not be happily ever after. 600-800 words.

Day 1:

they came today. the woods whispered their arrival, tiny feet treading on my heart. i saw them, eyes wide, mouths agape. "why do you live here, all alone?" the girl, Gretel, her voice dripping with a child's cruel innocence. i wanted to tell her of the villages that cast me out, of the whispers that follow a woman alone, of the magic that was my salvation and my curse. but i smiled, a spider's smile. "i enjoy the quiet," i said.

Day 3:

Hansel, he's clever, cleverer than he knows. he sees things. "why are your windows made of sugar?" he asked, a frown creasing his brow. i laughed, a sound like cracking ice. "to sweeten the view," i replied. but his eyes, they darted to the cages, the oven. suspicion grows. i see myself in him, the before me, the girl who questioned, who doubted, who was cast out. i must be careful.

Day 4:

i told them stories today, of witches and woods, of magic dark and light. "are you a witch?" Gretel asked, her voice a whisper of silk and steel. i hesitated, a fatal pause. "once, perhaps," i admitted. "but the world is not kind to witches." they nodded, the wisdom of the outcast in their young eyes. we are the same, in ways they cannot yet understand.

Day 5:

the plan, it curdles in my stomach like sour milk. i need the magic, to survive, to protect. but at what cost? i watch them, Hansel with his sharp, darting glances, Gretel, with her quiet, simmering strength. i see their futures, bright and burning. can i extinguish that light? "we want to go home," Hansel said today, his voice small. "soon," i lied, a serpent's promise. my heart, a stone in my chest.

Day 6:

i cannot. the oven looms, a dark promise. i see my reflection in its glass, a monster. "what are you going to do to us?" Gretel's voice, a blade. i falter. "nothing," i whisper, the word a key, unlocking something within me. "i will do nothing to you." they do not trust, but they see, perhaps, a glimmer of something else. redemption? no, not for me. but perhaps, a reprieve.

Day 7:

i taught them magic today. simple spells, child's play. but their eyes, how they lit up! "why are you helping us?" Hansel, ever the skeptic. "because," i said, searching for words as elusive as smoke, "because everyone deserves a chance." a chance i was never given. they smile, and for a moment, the shadows recede.

Day 8:

they left today. i showed them the path, a winding way through the woods. "will we see you again?" Gretel asked, her hand in mine, a fleeting warmth. "perhaps," i said, knowing the lie for what it was. the world has no place for witches and wayward children. but in their eyes, i saw a spark, a promise of something more. "thank you," Hansel said, and in his voice, i heard forgiveness, or something like it.

Final Entry:

the house is silent now, the echoes of their laughter a ghost in the walls. i am alone, as i have always been. but something has shifted, a door opened, a path revealed. i see now, the magic, it is not in the spells, the potions, the power. it is in the choices we make, the hands we hold, the chances we give. i am a witch, yes. but perhaps, that is not all i am.

they will tell stories of me, of the witch in the candy house. let them. i know the truth, as do they, the children who walked into the woods and found a witch, and in finding her, found themselves.

i will not say this is a happily ever after. but it is an ending, of sorts. and perhaps, a beginning.

# Writing Prompt #
##################
In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. Include dialogue that is terse and revealing of character. Write with a keen sense of time and place. Capture realness and authenticity. 600-800 words, past tense.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
The bar was small and the light was dim. It was the kind of place that seemed to absorb sound rather than echo it. The soldier sat at the far end, his back to the wall, a position that allowed him to see the door and everyone in the room. He had a beer in front of him, untouched, the foam sliding down the side of the glass like slow, lazy rivers.

He watched the door. People came and went. Some alone, some in pairs, laughing or arguing in low tones. He didn't know any of them. He didn't expect to. This town had changed while he was away, or maybe he was the one who had changed. It was hard to tell.

A man took the stool next to him, ordered a whiskey, neat. He was middle-aged, with a face that looked like it had seen its share of troubles. The soldier glanced at him, then away. Silence stretched between them, comfortable and familiar.

"You just get back?" the man finally asked, nodding at the soldier's jacket, which bore the insignia of a unit that had seen heavy fighting.

"Yeah." The soldier's answer was short, a single word that carried the weight of countless stories he wasn't ready to tell.

"I thought so. You've got that look."

"What look?"

"The one that says you're here but not really here. Seen it before."

The soldier considered this, took a sip of his beer. It tasted bitter. "Maybe."

The man chuckled, a low, raspy sound. "Yeah. Maybe. I remember that feeling. Like you're walking through a dream. Everything's the same but different."

The soldier looked at him now, really looked at him. "You served?"

"Long time ago. Different war, same shit."

They fell into silence again, the kind that spoke of shared understanding. The bartender replaced the man's empty glass without a word, the smooth motion of someone who had done this many times before.

"You find it hard?" the soldier asked after a while, not sure why he was opening up to this stranger.

"Every damn day." The man took a sip of his whiskey. "But it gets better. Or maybe you just get used to it."

The soldier nodded, not convinced. He took another sip of his beer, found it had lost its chill.

"See that girl over there?" The man nodded towards a young woman laughing at a table with friends. "That's my daughter. She was just a kid when I got back. Now look at her."

The soldier looked. She seemed vibrant, full of life. Something inside him ached at the sight.

"She knows?"

"About me? Yeah. But she doesn't understand. Not really. How could she?"

"Yeah." The soldier turned back to his beer. "How could anyone?"

They lapsed into silence again, the noise of the bar around them a distant buzz. The soldier thought about his own family, about the friends he had lost, about the things he had seen and done. It felt like a lifetime ago and just yesterday.

"You'll find your way," the man said, as if reading his thoughts. "Just give it time."

The soldier nodded, not sure if he believed him. But the words were kind, and kindness was a rare thing in the world he had come from.

The man finished his whiskey and stood up, dropping a few bills on the bar. "Take care of yourself."

"You too."

The man nodded, a gesture of mutual respect, and left. The soldier watched him go, then turned back to his beer. He felt a little less alone than he had before, a little more anchored to the world around him. It wasn't much, but it was a start.

He finished his beer and signaled for another. The bartender brought it over, and for the first time since he had walked in, the soldier smiled.

"Thanks."

"No problem. You staying long?"

The soldier thought about it, about the long road ahead, about the struggle to find his place in this new, old world.

"Maybe," he said. "Maybe I am."

Writing Prompt: Modern Epistolary Drama: Write a story told entirely through a series of text messages between two estranged siblings. The texts should span the course of several days and reveal a gradually unfolding family mystery. Use distinct voices for each character, and include details that provide insight into their personalities, motivations, and the complex history between them. The story should have an affecting but unresolved ending. The characters are a 40-year-old brother and 32-year-old sister. 600-800 words.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
**Day 1**

**Alex:** Hey, it’s been a while. How are you?

**Sam:** Busy. You?

**Alex:** Same. Listen, have you talked to Mom recently?

**Sam:** Not really. Why?

**Alex:** Something’s off. She’s been avoiding my calls. Last time we talked, she sounded... worried.

**Sam:** Probably nothing. You know how she gets.

**Alex:** Maybe. But, can you check on her? For me?

**Sam:** Fine. But only because you asked.

---

**Day 2**

**Sam:** Went to see Mom. She’s fine. Just busy with her garden club.

**Alex:** Did she say anything... unusual?

**Sam:** Not really. Why? What’s going on, Alex?

**Alex:** I found Dad’s old letters. They mention something... something hidden.

**Sam:** Hidden? What are you talking about?

**Alex:** I don’t know yet. Thought you might help me figure it out.

**Sam:** This sounds like one of your wild goose chases.

**Alex:** Please, Sam. It’s important.

**Sam:** *sigh* Fine. What do you need?

---

**Day 3**

**Sam:** I asked Mom about the letters. She denied everything.

**Alex:** Of course, she did. Did you find anything in Dad’s study?

**Sam:** Yeah. A locked drawer. Couldn’t open it.

**Alex:** I might know where the key is. Remember our secret hideout?

**Sam:** You think it’s there? After all these years?

**Alex:** Worth a shot.

---

**Day 4**

**Sam:** Found the key. And the drawer... Alex, there’s a lot here.

**Alex:** Like?

**Sam:** Papers. Old photos. And a deed for some property?

**Alex:** Property? Where?

**Sam:** Someplace upstate. Never heard of it.

**Alex:** We should check it out.

**Sam:** We? As in together?

**Alex:** Yeah. Like old times.

**Sam:** ...Okay. When?

---

**Day 5**

*No messages exchanged.*

---

**Day 6**

**Alex:** Tomorrow. I’ll pick you up.

**Sam:** Fine. But Alex, what if we don’t like what we find?

**Alex:** We’ll cross that bridge when we get to it.

---

**Day 7**

*On the road.*

**Sam:** This place is in the middle of nowhere.

**Alex:** Just a bit further.

---

*At the property.*

**Sam:** It’s a cabin. Looks abandoned.

**Alex:** Let’s go inside.

---

**Inside the cabin.**

**Sam:** Alex, look at this.

**Alex:** What is it?

**Sam:** Letters. Between Dad and someone named Marie.

**Alex:** Who’s Marie?

**Sam:** I don’t know. But these are love letters, Alex.

**Alex:** What? That can’t be.

**Sam:** It’s all here. Dates, times... even plans to meet here.

**Alex:** This doesn’t make sense. Dad loved Mom.

**Sam:** Maybe... Maybe there’s more to their story than we knew.

**Alex:** What do we do with this?

**Sam:** I don’t know. Confront Mom?

**Alex:** And say what? “Hey, we found Dad’s secret love nest”?

**Sam:** We need to talk to her. Together.

**Alex:** Yeah. Together.

---

**Day 8**

**Sam:** Mom agreed to see us. Tomorrow.

**Alex:** How did she sound?

**Sam:** Nervous. I think she knows why we want to talk.

**Alex:** This is going to change everything, isn’t it?

**Sam:** Probably. But maybe it’s time for the truth.

**Alex:** Yeah. Thanks, Sam. For doing this with me.

**Sam:** Always, Alex. No matter what.

---

**Day 9**

*The day of the meeting.*

*No messages exchanged.*

---

**Day 10**

**Alex:** I can’t believe it. All these years, and we never knew.

**Sam:** Mom was just trying to protect us. Protect Dad’s memory.

**Alex:** But at what cost? The lies, the secrets...

**Sam:** I know. It’s a lot to process.

**Alex:** What now?

**Sam:** We move forward. Together.

**Alex:** There’s still so much we don’t know.

**Sam:** Maybe some mysteries are better left unsolved.

**Alex:** Maybe. But I’m glad we did this. Together.

**Sam:** Me too, Alex. Me too.

*The siblings are left with more questions than answers, their relationship strengthened yet burdened by the weight of their family’s hidden past. The truth about their father, and how their mother fits into the narrative, remains partially uncovered, leaving them to navigate the complexities of family secrets and the bonds that keep them tied together.*

##################
# Writing Prompt #
##################
Fairy Tale Retelling: Rewrite the story of Hansel and Gretel from the perspective of the witch, in the format of raw, terse stream-of-consciousness diary entries written in her style & voice. She may at times be an unreliable narrator. She sees herself as fundamentally good and portrays herself sympathetically; she believes she is misunderstood and has a tragic backstory. Include snippets of dialogue between the witch and the children in a way that feels natural for a diary entry. You may take liberties with the original story. The witch will not die in this version; she needs to be able to write her final entry. It will not be happily ever after. 600-800 words.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
**Entry 1: The Children Arrive**

Two little rascals, they are. Why must fate send them to my door? I’ve heard tales of their wretched life under the care of their own flesh and blood. “Witch,” they call me. As if that’s all there is to a person. I’ve seen their gaunt faces through the window of my cottage, their eyes hollow, begging for something more than the scraps they’re given.

“Come in, my dears,” I say, though my heart is heavy with the burden of their need.

“We’re hungry,” the boy, Hansel, admits, his voice a whisper of desperation.

“And scared,” Gretel adds, her hand clutching her brother’s with a tremor.

**Entry 2: A Heart’s Decision**

I’ve taken them in, not out of malice, but out of pity. My own heart is barren, having been stripped by the cold hands of fate. These children remind me of the ones I once had—bright-eyed, curious, filled with life.

“You’ll stay with me until things can be sorted out,” I tell them, my voice a mix of determination and dread.

But what am I to do? The village folk whisper of their fate. They see only the worst in me, while I see only the worst in their situation.

“You mustn’t worry, dears,” I assure them, though my own mind is swirling with what-ifs. “We’ll keep this a secret, just between us.”

**Entry 3: A Misunderstood Hospitality**

They call me a witch, but what I offer these children is warmth and shelter, the basics that their own flesh and blood deny them.

“Sit by the fire, children,” I say, my voice soft, trying to erase the hard edges of my words.

“Tell me, Hansel, what do you like to do?”

“I like to run,” he says, his eyes lighting up for a moment.

“And you, Gretel?”

“I like to help in the kitchen,” she replies, her small hand reaching out to touch the warmth of the oven.

“Then you shall help me bake today,” I say, my heart twisting at the innocence in their words.

**Entry 4: A Twist of Fate**

I’ve heard whispers again, louder this time. The village is abuzz with tales of my nefarious deeds. How dare they judge me? I am no monster.

“You mustn’t go outside today,” I tell the children. “People are talking, and I don’t want you to hear the bad things they say about us.”

“Are we in trouble?” Hansel asks, his face creasing with worry.

“No, not at all,” I lie. “We’re just staying in today. It’s going to rain, anyways.”

**Entry 5: A Desperate Gamble**

Their presence in my home has drawn the attention of the village. They want to take the children away, to place them in the care of others. But what other care have they known?

“You must understand, children,” I say, my voice trembling with a mix of fear and resolve. “You must stay with me until I can find a better solution.”

“Will we ever see our family again?” Gretel asks, her voice breaking the silence.

“I don’t know, dear,” I admit, my heart sinking.

**Entry 6: A Bargain Made**

The village elders have come for the children. They stand at the door, their eyes wide with fear, clutching each other’s hands.

“Please, let me keep them,” I beg, my voice quavering. “I can give them a better life than anyone else can.”

“You’ve led them astray, witch,” one elder says, his face set in lines of disdain.

“I’ve only tried to help them,” I retort, my anger flaring. “You’ve never seen the depth of their despair until it’s too late.”

**Entry 7: The Parting**

The elders take the children, despite my pleas. As they leave, Hansel glances back, his eyes tear-streaked, searching for a moment of understanding or a flicker of hope.

“I’m sorry, my dears,” I whisper after them, my words chased by the cold wind.

**Entry 8: Alone Again**

They’re gone now, taken from me under the cloak of righteousness. I am alone again, as I always seem to be. But at least they’re safe, aren’t they?

**Final Entry: A New Understanding**

I’ve heard the whispers, the tales of misfortune that have befallen Hansel and Gretel since they left my care. They speak of starvation, of neglect—a cycle that I tried to break.

Perhaps, in my own way, I am a witch. A witch of misguided intentions, caught in a world that sees only the surface of things.

As I sit by the fire, the embers dying down, I realize that perhaps no one can save these children from the world, or save the world from itself.

I am not a monster. But perhaps, in the end, we are all just creatures of our own making, doomed to repeat the cycles we can never break. And in that stark truth, there lies neither a beginning nor an end, but only the endless turning of the wheel.

In the heart of the Roman Empire, where the sun seemed to hang perpetually in the sky, casting long shadows over the sand-sculpted arenas, I, Lucius Varro, stood as one of the empire's chosen instruments of spectacle and subjugation. My days flowed one into another, each indistinguishable from the last, marked only by the change in the crowds that gathered to watch the games.

The crowd roared like the sea, their voices a constant, thundering background to the spectacle. Each face in the crowd was a mask, a reflection of the empire's vast, diverse population. Here were the citizens of Rome, come to watch their lives played out in the arena: the violence, the brutality, the raw, unchecked power that was the Roman way.

As I waited in the shadows of the arena, the reality of my situation settled over me with the weight of the sun above. Here in the arena, I was not Lucius Varro, slave to the will of others, but a figure of fear and reverence.

I thought of my friend, Titus, who had been taken from his village as a boy, just as I had. He had been a shepherd, his life quiet and simple, until the day the recruiters came. Now, he too stood in the arena, a shadow among the other gladiators, each of us a testament to the empire's might and its ruthless disregard for human life.

The crowd's roar grew louder, a signal that the day's first event was to commence. I stepped out into the sunlight, my body armored in the traditional gladiator attire: a helmet that obscured my face, a breastplate that hugged my chest, and greaves to protect my legs. My weapon, a short sword, felt like an extension of my own hand, its weight familiar and comforting.

As I walked the perimeter of the arena, the crowd’s attention followed me. They chanted my name, a strange feeling that washed over me. Here in the arena, I was not just a man, but a character in a grand story, a figure of myth and lore already beginning to outlive the man within the armor. 

The game was a spectacle of survival, not just against my opponent, but against the very system that had bound me. As I awaited my opponent, I allowed my thoughts to wander to the fringes of the empire, to the lands beyond the reach of Rome. There, I imagined a life unbound by the iron grip of the arena, where I could be more than a gladiator, more than a tool of the empire.

Suddenly, the gate opened, and my opponent stepped into the arena. He was a large man, a slave from the northern realms, his skin marked with the tattoos of his tribe. We approached each other, the crowd’s roar building to a crescendo. Here, at the heart of Rome, we would fight not just for our lives, but for the spectators’ favor, for the entertainment of the elite.

As we circled each other, the tension in the air was palpable, thick as the dust that coated every surface of the arena. Our swords clashed, sparks flying like fleeting omens from the gods. Each strike was a prayer, each block a plea for mercy from the deities we both served.

In the midst of the battle, I caught a glimpse of the crowd's faces, a blur of expressions ranging from horror to delight. In that moment, I understood the dual nature of our role. We were both the monsters and the heroes in the eyes of the onlookers. We were the vessels through which they lived vicariously through acts of violence and courage.

Finally, with a swift motion, I disarmed my opponent, and the crowd's roar peaked, a thunderous celebration of my victory. As my opponent was led from the arena, I felt a mixture of relief and sorrow. Relief that I had survived, but sorrow for the life that had been lost, a life much like my own, bound up in the same cruel machinery of the empire.

As I walked back to my cell, the weight of the day's events settled heavily on my shoulders. Yet, even in the shadow of the arena’s walls, I could not help but feel a strange sense of pride. Today, I had not only fought for my life but for the dream of a life beyond the arena's reach. In the heart of Rome, beneath the shadow of Mars, I fought not only for myself but for all of us who were bound by the chain of the empire.

And in that thought, the dusty road seemed a little less daunting, the crowd’s roar a little less deafening, and the path to freedom a little less obscured.

# Writing Prompt #
##################
In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. Include dialogue that is terse and revealing of character. Write with a keen sense of time and place. Capture realness and authenticity. 600-800 words, past tense.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
In the back room of the bar, where the light from the neon sign flickered through the dust, the soldier sat alone. His name was Joe, or Joseph Anthony when he was younger and believed in such things. Now, he was simply the man at the end of the bar, the one with the left hand that didn’t quite work right, the one that trembled a little when he raised it to take a sip of his beer.

Joe had been in Italy, then Korea, and now somewhere in the jungles of a place he couldn’t even pronounce. He had seen things, done things, and carried the weight of them all in his chest, where they pressed down on his heart with every heartbeat.

The bar was quiet, the kind of quiet that comes from people knowing better than to disturb the solitude of someone who looks like they’ve seen hell and back. The only sound was the low hum of conversation from the other patrons, and the occasional clink of glasses.

A young man, no older than twenty-five, with a face that still held the promise of youth, approached Joe. He slid into the seat beside him, not because he wanted to share Joe’s company, but because the seat next to the old man was unoccupied and he was too lazy to walk further.

“Buy you a drink?” he asked, his voice carrying a cockiness that was typical of young men who haven’t yet had their illusions shattered.

Joe looked at him, his eyes narrowing slightly. “Whiskey. Straight.”

The young man nodded and flagged down the bartender. When the drink was set before Joe, the young man sat back, watching as Joe took a long sip, the amber liquid disappearing into the depths of his glass.

“You a soldier?” the young man asked, curiosity getting the better of his cockiness.

Joe’s gaze met his, direct and unyielding. “Was. Now I’m just a guy who likes his whiskey.”

“Sorry, sir. I didn’t mean—”

“It’s alright,” Joe cut him off, the edge in his voice softened by a sigh. “Just not much for talking about it.”

The young man nodded, understanding settling over his face. He took a drink, then, feeling the tension in the air, decided it was best to change the subject. “So, what’s the war like?”

Joe’s hand paused in the act of lifting his glass. It trembled slightly, the scars on his knuckles standing out in stark relief in the dim light. “It’s like hell, son. Only hotter.”

“You been in a lot of fights?”

“More than I can count,” Joe replied, his voice flatter than the surface of his drink. “But you learn real quick that every fight is the same. It’s just the faces that change.”

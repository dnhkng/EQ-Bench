# Model: gemini-ultra

##################
# Writing Prompt #
##################
Fairy Tale Retelling: Rewrite the story of Hansel and Gretel from the perspective of the witch, in the format of raw, terse stream-of-consciousness diary entries written in her style & voice. She may at times be an unreliable narrator. She sees herself as fundamentally good and portrays herself sympathetically; she believes she is misunderstood and has a tragic backstory. Include snippets of dialogue between the witch and the children in a way that feels natural for a diary entry. You may take liberties with the original story. The witch will not die in this version; she needs to be able to write her final entry. It will not be happily ever after. 600-800 words.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
**Day 1**

The woods feel lonely. Cold. Mama used to say witches turn cruel when left alone too long. Didn't believe her then. Starting to now. Curse this gnarled body and these cravings only a child's soul can satisfy. Must resist. Will resist. 

Maybe the forest will offer something. Acorns are for squirrels, berries poisonous this time of year. Nothing, always nothing. Just like my wretched stomach. I built the house to lure birds, silly things peck at the sugar but won't come close enough. Not like children would. 

**Day 3**

Voices. Little, high voices getting closer. Two of them. A boy and a girl, ragged coats, faces gaunt but eyes still bright. The girl holds his hand, the fierce way big sisters do. The ache in my chest is worse than hunger. I hide. Watch.

"Look at it, Hansel! All made of sweets." The girl sounds greedy, not scared. Witch heart jumps. They're orphans. Or runaways. Lost and at my doorstep. Mama was right. Loneliness curdles even a good witch.

**Day 4**

They ate until their bellies were full. Slept like angels on the pile of furs meant for my own cold bones. This morning, they left. Ungrateful brats. Didn't even say thank you.  Loneliness claws at me again. I peek out the window. Snow falls thick. No child could find their way home in that. It's for their own good. They'll come back. 

**Day 6**

They did! Back at the door, whining now. "Please, kind lady, we're so lost." The girl does that hand-holding thing again, brother snivels behind her. They'll break my weak will. I let them in, but this time there's a catch.  My house, my rules. 

They need to earn their keep. I show them the broom, the bucket. "Chores," I rasp, "In exchange for food and shelter." They do a sorry job of it. Floors still sticky, crumbs everywhere. Worse, they eye the big oven, whispering, giggling. Those are witch-eating eyes.

**Day 8**

"Help me gather herbs, little ones." I take them deep into the woods, the kind of place mothers warn about. The girl looks sharp, keeps tugging her brother back from the darkest patches. 

Found enough for soup, but not enough…not enough for what my belly demands. Not enough to stop the whispers in my head. I don't want to, but Mama's voice echoes: *Cruelty, child, is born of need.*

They suspect. I see it in their eyes. Found a trail of shiny pebbles behind the house. Smart girl. Not smart enough. This time, deeper into the woods we'll go. 

**Day 10**

The boy cries. The girl trembles but doesn't let go of his hand. The witch pretends she doesn't hear them. They should've run while they could. Foolish children, believing the world is kind. Haven't they learned anything?

"We're lost," the boy wails.

"Of course you are," the witch mutters and turns her back on them.

**Day 12**

Can't find them. The forest swallowed them whole or they've stumbled upon a village kinder than mine. Hunger twists my insides sharper than guilt. I did what I had to. Didn't I? Mama wouldn't blame me.

The house feels too big. Too sweet. Nibble the corner of a candy windowpane, spit it out. Not the cure I was hoping for. 

**Day 15**

Something gleams by the creek. A golden locket, heart-shaped. The girl's. Dropped it while running, no doubt. Proof they didn't get away clean. Or proof the witch isn't as monstrous as she thinks.

I tuck the locket in my pocket, finger the cold metal. It's all I have of them now, those children who dared to taste my house, my heart. Loneliness is a powerful hunger. Not even sweets can satisfy it.

**Final Entry**

The village is near. I smell woodsmoke, hear dogs bark. Should've gone there before. Before the children, before the…incident. They'll call me a monster, the children will. If they survived.

No matter. This diary, they'll find it. Read my witchy scrawl. Maybe then they'll understand. A good heart starved can turn monstrous. A full belly, though? That never made anyone a hero.

# Writing Prompt #
##################
Historical Fiction: Write a scene from a story set during the height of the Roman Empire, focusing on a slice of a day in the life of a gladiator. Do not write a combat scene. Use sensory details to capture the sights, sounds, and smells of ancient Rome. Explore the gladiator's thoughts and emotions. The story should also touch on the larger political and social issues of the time period. The piece should feel like a slice of a larger story. First person, past tense, 600-800 words.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
My knuckles ached as I unlaced the leather gauntlet, the hardened hide rubbing against raw flesh. I closed my eyes, inhaling the pungent musk of sweat-soaked bodies mixed with the lingering iron tang of blood. Not my own, at least not today. The air thrummed with the dull roar of the distant crowd, a constant reminder of my fate, but today it felt more like a background hum than a death knell. A sense of restless anticipation crackled within me, a hunger that went deeper than the gnawing emptiness in my belly. I’d won today, a brutal affair that had me facing two seasoned veterans desperate for a return to glory. The sting of their blades was a small price to pay for survival. 

I opened my eyes and surveyed the barracks. Bodies lay scattered across the straw-strewn floor - battered, exhausted, or in an irreversible sleep.  A young Celt, new to this place, stared out the barred window, tears tracing silent paths down his filth-streaked face. A Thracian snored, the rise and fall of his chest thankfully unmarred by the rasp of a death rattle. We were a brotherhood forged not by blood, but by the fickle hand of fate and the insatiable thirst of the Roman mob for blood.

The lanista – master of our fates – strode into the barracks, his gait the rolling swagger of a man untroubled by the suffering around him. His eyes, the color of tarnished coins, settled on me.  "Varro," he barked, "The Emperor himself requests your presence."  

A tremor coursed through me, but it wasn't fear, or at least not solely fear. To be summoned by the Emperor, the most powerful man in the known world, the one whose word could make me a champion or snuff out my life with a dismissive gesture, that was an opportunity. A chance to tear myself from the endless, grinding cycle of sweat, blood, and sand. 

I followed the lanista through the shadowed corridors, the smooth marble a stark contrast to the rough-hewn rock of our living quarters. As a slave, a prisoner of Rome, and a gladiator, I was the lowest of the low. But in the arena, for those precious heartbeats during a match, we were more than brute animals. We were the living embodiment of courage, skill, and above all, the whims of the fickle gods.  

We emerged into the blinding heat of the day. The shouts of the crowd washed over me; it was a sound that both filled me with a primal thrill and set my teeth on edge. Today, they roared for others. But perhaps, after today, they might roar for me. Ahead rose the magnificent structure of the Colosseum, a testament to Roman ambition and cruelty carved into gleaming white stone. The reek of wild animals, a blend of predator musk and sour fear, drifted from the arched entryways beneath the vast tiered stands. 

My weapon of choice, the gladius, rested in its familiar weight against my thigh. It was a simple blade, short and double-edged, but in my hands, it was an extension of my will. My father had once wielded the same weapon, not in the arena, but during the campaign that saw him dragged back to Rome in chains. My mother had hidden it after his death, whispering its name, “Deliverance,” as tears fell onto the cold steel. It would be my deliverance now. Not from death – that was the one certainty for a gladiator – but from this wretched half-life spent in the shadows.

The lanista led me away from the arena. We ventured deeper into the city, past the raucous shouts of merchants and the plaintive cries of beggars, to the Palatine Hill, heart of the Empire. I gazed around in awe, taking in the opulent villas, their gardens a riot of color, and the grand temples, where the sweet smoke of burnt offerings spiraled towards the impassive heavens. Slaves, their movements as unobtrusive as their faces were blank, scurried about their duties. It struck me then how much Rome was built upon their chained backs, just as my own small victories relied on the broken bodies of my fellows within the barracks.

I was nothing. I was everything. 

We arrived at the Imperial Palace, its towering walls an intimidating testament to absolute power. Each polished stone whispered of the whims of the emperors who resided here, men who could raise armies and topple nations with a flicker of their will. As the lanista led me through gilded halls lined with silent guards, I thought of Claudius, the so-called fool, poisoned by his own wife, and Nero, the madman who saw fit to fiddle while his capital burned. 

Perhaps my own rise was not so impossible after all. Perhaps in the eyes of the Emperor, I, a nameless son of a conquered people, was a distraction, a novelty amidst the endless games of power and intrigue. Yet, novelty could become legend, and survival in the arena had taught me the value of seizing opportunities when the gods saw fit to toss them my way.

# Writing Prompt #
##################
Epistolary Narrative: Write a story told entirely through a series of brief correspondences: telegrams or letters or emails between two characters. The correspondence should span several months or years, and reveal a gradually unfolding plot. Use distinct voices for each character, and include details that provide insight into their personalities and motivations. The story should build to an emotional climax, and the final letter should provide a satisfying resolution. The setting is a lighthouse keeper writing to his mother. He is working class and scottish. # Test Model Output #

**27 July 1895**

Dearest Ma,

Forgive the scribble, it's the damned roll of this tub out here heading north. Mull of Kintyre's a fine landmark, but all this water gives me a head like a cracked egg. They've set me on the Skerryvore, Ma. Towering thing, shining eyes out over the Atlantic like it means to search for God himself. Bit grand for a lad like me, wouldn't you say?

They gave me a book of Burns. Said it might keep me company. I told the Captain I'd rather have a fiddle and a dram of good whisky. 

Ma, it's lonely. 

Your lad always,
Alasdair

**11 August 1895**

Ma,

Got your parcel of oatcakes. Reckon a weevil found its way into one (or else it's the salt air making my brain turn). The gulls here have voices like angry drunks. One stole clean away with my breakfast roll this morning, feathers and all.

Tell young Tam I said to quit his blubbering about schoolwork. There's worse lessons to be learned out here, with the wind howling round this tower and the seagulls screaming.

Your Alasdair

**5 September 1895**

Dearest Ma,

The storms hit hard these past weeks. I've not had a good night's rest, thinking the waves will drag Skerryvore and me right down to the ocean floor. The other keeper, an old salt named McDougal, says I've got the look of a sailor on his first voyage. Gives me tales to pass the nights – smugglers, mermaids, the lot. Truth and tall tales, likely mixed in equal measure.  

I try to fill the time, Ma. Clean the lens, tend the light. Burns is by my cot, the pages getting stiff with the damp. Tried reading a bit, but his fancy words go all muddled in my head out here. 

Miss you all dearly,
Alasdair

**6 November 1895**

Ma,

McDougal is teaching me knots. Never had the patience before, but the hours are long, and the wind carries a chill now that makes the bones ache. I dreamed of the old cottage last night. The warmth of the hearth, Tam and Ellie squabbling… God, I'd give a month's pay to sit at the kitchen table again. I can almost taste your broth.

Winters are harsh on the Skerryvore, Ma. I'm not sure I've the stomach for it.

Your Alasdair

**3 February 1896**

Ma,

I saw a ship run aground in the storms. Just a glimpse through the rain, then it was swallowed by the waves. McDougal says nothing's to be done. We signal the mainland, but on nights like that, there's no souls brave enough to launch a rescue. All we do is wait for what washes ashore.

Sometimes I think the Skerryvore will be my tomb, Ma.

Forgive me. Shouldn't be putting such thoughts on you. 

Your Alasdair

**4 May 1896**

Dearest Ma,

A calm has settled, thank the Lord. McDougal caught me napping on watch yesterday, gave me a right scolding. Can't say he's wrong. Head’s gone to wool out here. He brought me a little box, full of odds and ends. Says it's to tinker with, keep the mind busy when there's no storms to battle. Bits of wire, polished stones, an old compass with the needle bent askew. 

I'm sending you a pebble I found on the shore. Smooth as an egg, washed up from God knows where. Keep it safe for me.

Your Alasdair

**6 October 1896**

Ma,

They sent a new keeper to relieve McDougal.  A young one, barely old enough to shave.  McDougal said there's no shame in admitting the sea isn't for everyone. He gave me a fierce handshake, and clapped me on the shoulder. Said I stuck it out longer than most.

He left me the tinker's box. I reckon all the keepers of the Skerryvore leave something behind. I think I'll carve a wee boat from driftwood, add it to the collection.

The tender takes me back to the mainland in the morning. Maybe I'll find work on the docks, or on one of the fishing boats.  It'll feel good to have solid earth beneath my feet again, Ma.

I'm coming home.

Your loving son,
Alasdair

# Writing Prompt #
##################
In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. Include dialogue that is terse and revealing of character. Write with a keen sense of time and place. Capture realness and authenticity. 600-800 words, past tense.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
The joint reeked. Sweat, stale beer, and that sour note of too many bodies jammed too close. Didn't matter if it was a barroom back home or some nameless dive overseas. Same smell, just a different brand of rot in the corners. 

My boots felt like concrete weights. Every muscle ached. I hunched at the bar and ordered a whiskey. One shot, neat. The bartender was a weathered old bird, face creased like a map. He poured, didn't even blink at my uniform when I slapped some coins down.  I knocked it back. The burn was good. Something to cut through the fog.

The place was crowded. GIs on leave, their laughter too loud. A few girls, painted up like they were on parade. But mostly, it was the locals. The ones who didn't get shipped out, who just kept drinking, waiting for something to happen. 

"You seen action?"

The voice came from beside me, a guy in a worn flannel shirt and jeans. Probably a factory worker. Didn't look old enough to shave, but there was a hollowness in his eyes I'd seen before.

"Enough," I said. I ordered another. 

"What's it like?" He leaned in, breath smelling of stale cigarettes.

"You ever step on a rotten apple?" I asked him. "That squishy pop and the stink that sticks to your shoe? It's kinda like that."

He looked startled, like I'd slapped him. Maybe he was expecting some hero tale. Instead, I just tossed back more whiskey. It didn't taste like much. Didn't feel like much either.

The flannel kid was still hanging around. Staring. 

"Can't you take a hint, buddy?" I said. "I ain't got no stories for you."

He flushed, mumbled some apology, and slunk off. 

I signaled for another round. The old bartender watched me. Didn't try to make conversation, which was just fine. Some nights, you just want the silence. Or as close as you can get to it with the jukebox warbling in the corner.

Didn't know how long I'd been there. Too long, most likely. My head felt thick and heavy, and the room was starting to spin a bit. The faces, the noise, it all swirled together into one big, ugly mess. Just like always. I fumbled some change out, left more than I should have. 

Leaving, I stumbled once. The old bartender caught my eye, a flicker of something like pity in there. But he didn't say a word. I was used to that too. 

Outside, the night air slapped me awake. Sort of. Cold cut through the whiskey buzz. I stood on the curb, watching the cars smear by. Headlights, taillights, all blending together. Some of them heading home, I figured. 

I didn't have a home. Not anymore. Sure, there was the house where I grew up. My folks were still there. They'd hug me when I showed up, my mother would cry. But it wasn't the same. Nothing would ever be the same.

"Hell of a world, ain't it?" I muttered to myself. Didn't know if I even believed it, but saying it was something. 

I started walking. One foot in front of the other. The only way I knew how. The war, it wasn't just out there in the trenches. It was in here, in your guts, in the way you saw things slantwise when nobody else did. 

Maybe a hot meal would help. Maybe a long walk would clear my head. Maybe some sleep. But the truth, the one the whiskey couldn't wash out, was there was no fixing this. Some things, once broken, they just stayed that way. I'd carry it, this weight. Always. That was what they didn't tell you about being a soldier, being a survivor. The battle never really ended.

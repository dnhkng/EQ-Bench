In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. The bar was dim, smelled of stale beer and old smoke. A long mirror behind the counter reflected rows of dusty bottles, their labels faded. He sat at the far end, nursing a whiskey. The ice clinked against the glass as he lifted it, his hand steady. He'd learned that steadiness a long time ago, along with other things best forgotten.

The bartender, a heavyset man with a bald head and a stained apron, wiped down the counter with a rag. He glanced at the soldier, then looked away. The soldier was used to that. People didn't know what to make of him, with his short-cropped hair and the scar that ran like a pale worm across his cheek. He didn't mind. He didn't much know what to make of himself these days.

"Another one?" the bartender asked, his voice gruff.

The soldier nodded. The whiskey burned going down, a familiar warmth spreading through his gut. It helped to dull the edges, the memories that lurked like shadows at the corners of his mind.

Two men came in, their voices loud, their laughter echoing in the emptiness of the bar. They wore suits, their faces flushed, their ties loosened. Businessmen, the soldier thought. They sat a few stools down, slapping each other on the back, talking about deals and money.

He listened, a wry smile playing on his lips. Deals. Money. Those things seemed so far removed from the world he'd known, the world of mud and blood, of fear and death. He'd seen things, done things, that these men couldn't even imagine. He'd seen the darkness that lived in the hearts of men, and the light too, sometimes, flickering like a candle flame in the wind.

"Heard they're sending more troops over," one of the businessmen said, his voice slurring slightly. "Damn fools. What's the point?"

The soldier took a sip of his whiskey, the ice clinking against his teeth. He knew the point. He'd seen it up close, felt it in his bones. The point was survival, the fight for another breath, another day. The point was the man next to you, the one you'd die for, the one who'd die for you.

"War's good for business," the other man said, his voice thick with cynicism. "Always has been."

The soldier looked at him, his gaze steady. He saw the man's eyes flicker, a brief moment of discomfort, then look away. He knew that look. He'd seen it in the eyes of the enemy, the fear hidden behind a mask of bravado.

"Yeah, good for business," the soldier said, his voice low, a rasping whisper. "For some."

He finished his drink, the ice melting in the empty glass. He stood up, his movements stiff, his body aching with old wounds. He tossed a few bills on the counter, enough to cover the drinks and a little more.

"Keep the change," he said to the bartender, who nodded silently.

He walked out of the bar, into the cool night air. The street was deserted, the only sound the distant hum of traffic. He looked up at the sky, a vast expanse of darkness dotted with stars. He wondered if they looked the same over there, in that place he'd left behind, the place that would forever be a part of him.

He started walking, his footsteps echoing on the pavement. He didn't know where he was going, but he knew one thing: he was home. And that, for now, was enough.

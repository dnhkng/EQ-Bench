In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. He sat alone, nursing his drink at the end of the bar. The light caught the lined face, deep creases etched into the skin like the maps he once followed. The bartender, a burly man with a bald head, wiped the counter, eyes following the soldier's every move.

"Long day, soldier?" the bartender asked.

The soldier grunted, not rising to look at the man. "Longer."

"I don't do tricks," the soldier said, taking a sip.

"War changes you," the bartender said suddenly, breaking the silence.

"You've seen things," the bartender continued. "I've heard stories. But I can't imagine."

"I reckon that's saying something."

"Yeah." The soldier drained his glass, feeling the burn of the whiskey on his throat. "It's all just a mess, isn't it?"

"Yeah," the bartender agreed, watching the soldier with an understanding gaze. "It is."

The soldier finished his drink, tossing the glass into the sink. He stood, the weight of his experiences clinging to him like a second skin.

"Maybe another time," he said, nodding at the bartender.

The bartender nodded back, returning to his work.

So, the soldier left the bar, another memory etched into his soul. He slid past the door, the noise of the world outside growing louder.

In the bar, life went on, but for him, it was a world he could no longer be part of. He walked into the shadows, carrying the burden of a soldier who had seen too much, understood too little, and now sought solace in the solitude of his own company.

The war had changed him, and no matter how hard he tried, he could never go back. <|end|>

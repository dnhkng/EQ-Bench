# Writing Prompt #
##################
In the style of Ernest Hemingway: Write a scene in a larger story about a soldier returning home from war. The scene is set in a bar. It should carry the jadedness of a seasoned combatant but with a wry humour. Write in Heminway's voice, naturally: Use short, declarative sentences and simple, straightforward language. Include dialogue that is terse and revealing of character. Write with a keen sense of time and place. Capture realness and authenticity. 600-800 words, past tense.

#####################
# Test Model Output #
#####################
The screen door slammed behind him as he entered the bar. The soldier, still in his uniform, looked out of place amongst the locals. His eyes, once bright and full of life, were now dull and lifeless. He took a seat at the counter, running a hand over his cropped hair. It was a habit he'd picked up in the army, a way to keep the hair out of his eyes during a firefight.

The bartender, a middle-aged man with a friendly face, greeted him with a nod. "What can I get you, soldier?"

The soldier sighed, "Just a beer, thanks."

As the bartender poured his drink, the soldier couldn't help but overhear the conversation at the table behind him. Two older men were discussing the war, their voices filled with anger and frustration.

"It's a damn shame, what they're doing to those boys over there," one of them said.

The soldier took a long pull from his beer, his mind wandering back to the battlefield. The sounds of gunfire and explosions echoed in his ears. The smell of smoke and blood still clung to his clothes.

"They're just kids, most of them," the other man replied. "They didn't sign up for this."

The soldier turned to face the men, his gaze steady. "I did," he said quietly.

The two men looked at him, surprise registering on their faces. The soldier took another sip of his beer, his eyes never leaving theirs.

"I signed up for this. I volunteered. I wanted to serve my country."

The first man spoke up, "But it's not right, what they're doing to you all. You deserve better."

The soldier shrugged, "Maybe. But it's my job. And I'm going to do it to the best of my ability."

The two men nodded, offering silent respect. The soldier turned back to his beer, his mind still on the battlefield.

As the night wore on, the bar began to fill up. Men and women, young and old, all gathered to drink and forget their troubles. The soldier remained at the counter, lost in his thoughts.

A young woman took the seat next to him, ordering a drink. She was beautiful, with long blonde hair and bright blue eyes. The soldier couldn't help but notice her, but he knew better than to make a move. He was a soldier, and she was civilian. They lived in different worlds.

"What's your story, soldier?" she asked, her voice soft and inviting.

The soldier hesitated, then decided to tell her the truth. "I've seen things," he said quietly. "Things that no one should have to see. I've done things, things that I'm not proud of."

The woman looked at him with understanding, "But you're home now. You're safe."

The soldier shook his head, "I don't know if I'll ever be safe again. I don't know if I'll ever be the same."

The woman reached out, placing a hand on his arm. "You will," she said with certainty. "You're stronger than you think."

The soldier looked at her, his eyes filled with gratitude. He knew she was right. He was a soldier, and he would always be a soldier. But he was also a man, and he was home.

As the night wore on, the soldier found himself opening up to the woman. He told her about the battles he'd fought, the friends he'd lost, and the things he'd seen. She listened, her eyes filled with compassion and understanding.

When the bar finally closed, the soldier walked the woman home. They stood on her doorstep, the silence between them heavy.

"Thank you," the soldier said quietly.

The woman smiled, "No, thank you. For sharing your story with me."

The soldier nodded, then turned and walked away. He knew he would never see her again, but he was grateful for the connection they'd made.

As he walked back to the barracks, the soldier couldn't help but feel a sense of peace. He was home, and he was alive. He knew that the road ahead would be long and difficult, but he was ready.

He was a soldier, and he would always be a soldier. But he was also a man, and he was home. And for now, that was enough.

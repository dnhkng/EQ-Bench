# Writing Prompt #
##################
Fairy Tale Retelling: Rewrite the story of Hansel and Gretel from the perspective of the witch, in the format of raw, terse stream-of-consciousness diary entries written in her style & voice. She may at times be an unreliable narrator. She sees herself as fundamentally good and portrays herself sympathetically; she believes she is misunderstood and has a tragic backstory. Include snippets of dialogue between the witch and the children in a way that feels natural for a diary entry. You may take liberties with the original story. The witch will not die in this version; she needs to be able to write her final entry. It will not be happily ever after. 600-800 words.

January 1st

Today I saw them again—those two children with the vacant eyes of hunger. Hansel and Gretel, they call themselves. I don't know why they keep coming back to my woods. I've tried to be kind, leave them breadcrumbs to find their way home, but they always return. Perhaps it's fate that brings them to me. Perhaps it's something more sinister.

January 5th

I can offer warmth, food, a place to rest their weary heads.

January 6th

They've found my house. I watched them from the shadows as they devoured everything in sight—the gumdrop roof, the licorice doorframe, even the sugar windowpanes. They're like animals. I had to intervene before they destroyed it all.

"Why do you eat my house?" I asked, stepping out into the light.

The girl, Gretel, looked up with those wide, frightened eyes. "We didn't know it was yours," she whispered.

"It doesn't matter," I said, trying to sound firm but kind. "You're safe here."

January 10th

January 15th

"What do you do with all these bones?" he asked today, pointing to the pile in the corner.

"They're just... remnants," I replied, avoiding his gaze. "From animals I've helped pass on to the next life."

January 20th

"Baking," I said, smiling sweetly. "Would you like to help me make some cookies?"

"Why, for you and your brother, of course," I said. "You both have such a sweet tooth."

"Tell us about your mother," Gretel said as we sat by the fire. "You never speak of her."

"She was... she was like you," I said, my voice cracking. "But she was taken from me."

"Taken?" Hansel asked. "By whom?"

"By the cruel world," I said, stoking the flames. "By those who didn't understand her gifts."

February 5th

I've decided to keep them. They're like pets now—obedient, dependent on me for their survival. They don't know it yet, but they're staying here forever. I've started to grow attached to them. February 10th

Hansel tried to escape last night—the little fool. He left a trail of pebbles this time. I followed it easily enough and brought him back. He's locked in his room now, crying for his sister. I'll deal with him later. For now, I need to figure out what to do with Gretel. She's too smart for her own good. I can't have her turning on me too.

"Why do you keep us here?" she demanded when I brought her food. "What do you want from us?"

"I just want to keep you safe," I said, my voice shaking. "The woods are dangerous."

"We can take care of ourselves," she spat back. "Let us go!"

"You don't understand," I whispered. "I need you."

February 15th

They're plotting something. I can feel it in the air—the tension, the whispers when they think I'm not listening. I've been watching them more closely today, trying to read their intentions. They're up to no good. I know it.

"What do you think she'll do to us?" Hansel asked his sister in hushed tones.

"I don't know," Gretel replied. "But we have to get out of here."

"How?"

"We'll find a way," she said, her voice filled with determination. "We'll outsmart her."

March 1st

March 5th

March 15th

March 20th

March 25th

April 5th

April 15th

I miss them. I miss their laughter, their questions, their endless appetites. I miss the way they looked at me with wonder—like I was something more than just a witch. I miss the way they needed me. I've tried to convince myself that I'm better off alone, but deep down I know it's not true. I'm just... empty.

April 20th

I saw them today—Hansel and Gretel. They were playing by the river's edge, their faces flushed with joy. They looked so happy—so free. April 25th

Maybe one day they'll understand that I never wanted to hurt them—that all I wanted was to be loved. Until then, I'll keep moving forward, one step at a time. Alone, as always.

May 1st

May 10th

The clangor of metal against metal fills the air as I parry and thrust with my fellow gladiators—each one striving to survive another day in the arena. The Colosseum looms over us like a great stone sentinel, a reminder that our fate is tied to the whims of the crowd and the emperor's thumb. His voice carries the weight of experience and authority, and we all know better than to ignore him. LETTER 1 (JANUARY)

Dearest Mother,

I hope this letter finds you in good health and high spirits. I've been at the lighthouse now for a month and I must admit the solitude is beginning to weigh on me. The sea is vast and unchanging, yet somehow it feels as though it's closing in around me with each passing day. I've taken to reading the books you sent in my free time to keep the loneliness at bay. Your care packages are a beacon in the fog of my days here.

Please give my love to Father and the siblings. Tell them I miss them dearly and that I'll write again soon.

Your devoted son,

Now, Billy didn't always have such monstrous mittens for feet. It all started when he was born with feet so tiny and delicate that his mother had to knit him socks out of spider silk to keep them warm. But as Billy grew, so did his feet, and by the time he was six, they had ballooned to their current size. His mother took him to every doctor and witch in the land, but none could offer a cure for his bumbling condition. The village children teased him mercilessly, calling him "Billy Bumblefoot" behind his back and laughing as he stumbled through the cobblestone streets.

One gloomy afternoon, as Billy was trying to deliver a loaf of bread to Old Lady Snozzle without dropping it, he stumbled upon a curious little shop at the end of the lane. It was nestled between two tall, crooked houses and had a sign above the door that read "Mr. Whipple's Wondrous Emporium of Extraordinary Things." Billy had never noticed the shop before, and his curiosity piqued like a cat spotting a dangling piece of string. He peered through the grimy window and saw shelves upon shelves of peculiar items: bottles filled with glittering potions, books with titles like "How to Tame Your Dragon in 3 Easy Steps," and candy that looked suspiciously like it might bite back if you weren't careful.

The door creaked open with a sound like a ghost clearing its throat, and Billy stepped inside. The shop was dimly lit with candles that flickered and danced in the draft from his oversized sneezes. A bell tinkled above the door as it closed behind him, and a man with a beard so long it could double as a mop emerged from the shadows.

"Welcome to my shop," Mr. Whipple said, his eyes twinkling like stars in a midnight sky. "What can I help you with today?"

Billy looked at his feet and sighed. "I wish I could be normal," he said. "I wish I didn't trip over everything and make such a mess."

Mr. Whipple leaned on his counter, which was carved from the trunk of an ancient tree and had more knobs and drawers than you could count. "Ah," he said knowingly. "You seek a remedy for your clumsiness." He rummaged through a drawer and pulled out a pair of shoes no larger than a mouse's ear. "These," he said, holding them up with a flourish, "are the Slippers of Sprightliness."

Billy's eyes widened like saucers. "What do they do?"

"These slippers," Mr. Whipple explained with a wink, "will shrink your feet to the size of a ladybug's and fill you with the grace of a gazelle."

Billy's heart leaped with excitement. "How do they work?"

"Simply wear them," Mr. Whipple said, "and whisper 'Sprightly Feet' three times before you go to sleep."

Billy couldn't believe his luck. He handed over his last three silver coins and dashed home, eager to try out his new shoes. That night, he whispered "Sprightly Feet" with all his might and slipped the tiny slippers onto his massive paws. Instantly, his feet began to shrink. They shrank and shrank until they were as dainty as a ballerina's. Billy giggled as he danced around his room on his tiptoes without knocking over a single thing.

The next morning, Billy woke up feeling lighter than a feather. He slipped out of bed and tiptoed downstairs without waking his snoring parents. He put on his new shoes and stepped outside into the crisp morning air. The village had never looked so inviting! He pranced down the lane, his feet barely touching the ground. The children who once taunted him now stared in amazement as he glided past them like a leaf on a breeze.

But alas, Billy's joy was short-lived. As he approached Old Lady Snozzle's house, he noticed something peculiar happening to his feet. They were growing again! And not just back to their usual size—they were growing even larger! By the time he reached her doorstep, his feet had swollen to the size of tractor tires. Panic set in as he tried to turn around and run, but his newfound grace had abandoned him. He stumbled and crashed through Mrs. Snozzle's garden gate, sending flowers flying like confetti at a wedding gone wrong.

The old woman peeked out her window and scowled. "Billy Bumblefoot!" she screeched. "What on earth have you done now?"

Billy looked at his feet in horror. "I don't know," he wailed. "I bought these shoes from Mr. Whipple!"

Mrs. Snozzle's eyes narrowed. "Ah," she said. "The Slippers of Sprightliness."

"They're supposed to make me graceful," Billy said, his voice trembling. "But they're making me grow!"

"They do indeed," she cackled. "But only at dawn. You see, Mr. Whipple has quite the sense of humor."

Billy felt his heart sink into his oversized sneakers. "What do I do?"

"You must return to Mr. Whipple and ask for the Antidote," she said. "But beware, Billy. He doesn't give it out for free."

Billy hobbled back to the Emporium as fast as his giant feet would allow. When he burst through the door, Mr. Whipple was polishing a crystal ball with his beard. "Ah," he said calmly. "You're back."

"Please," Billy begged. "I need the Antidote!"

Mr. Whipple chuckled. "I knew you would," he said. "But I can't just give it to you. You must first pass three tests to prove you're worthy."

Billy's heart sank even further. "What kind of tests?" he asked, his voice small and hopeful.

"The first," Mr. Whipple said, "is to navigate my maze without knocking over a single item." He gestured to a room in the back of the shop filled with precarious towers of china and glassware. "The second is to catch a greased piglet without using your hands." And with another gesture, he revealed a pen where a small, slippery piglet was rolling around in a tub of lard. "And the third," he said with a wicked grin, "is to tell me the most outrageous lie you can think of."

Billy took a deep breath and stepped into the maze. He tiptoed around the delicate objects, his oversized feet feeling like boulders in a china shop. He made it to the center with only one minor toe tap on a teacup. "Phew," he said to himself. "That wasn't so bad."

Next came the greased piglet. Billy watched as it shot out of the tub like a bar of soap in a bathtub and skidded across the floor. He took off his shirt and used it as a net to catch the giggling creature. It was messy, but he managed to do it without breaking a sweat—or any china.

The third test was the trickiest. Billy knew he wasn't much good at lying—his nose had been known to grow as long as his feet when he told even the tiniest fib. But he thought hard and finally blurted out, "I can juggle flaming torches with my feet!"

Mr. Whipple roared with laughter. "That's the spirit!" he said, handing Billy a tiny bottle labeled "Antidote." "One drop on each toe before you go to bed and your feet will return to their usual size."

Billy couldn't wait to get home and try it out. That night, he applied the Antidote as instructed and whispered "Sprightly Feet" again before drifting off to sleep. When he woke up, his feet were back to their usual size—not too big and not too small—just right for stomping through puddles and chasing after his friends.

But Billy had learned his lesson. He realized that being clumsy wasn't so bad after all. It made him who he was, and his friends liked him just the way he was—bumblefooted and all. And so Billy went about his days with a newfound confidence, his feet firmly planted on the ground—until one fateful evening when he spotted another curious object in Mr. Whipple's window: "The Helmet of Hilarity—Guaranteed to Make You Laugh at Any Situation."
